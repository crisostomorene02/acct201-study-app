Ch. 2 — Cost Behavior & Estimation Exam 1

Total Cost Equation
Total Cost = (Variable Cost per unit × # of units) + Fixed Cost
📌 What it means: Your total costs = the part that grows with volume (variable) PLUS the flat amount you always pay (fixed).
📌 When to use: Predicting costs at any activity level. Plug in a volume and it spits out total cost.
Example: VC = $5/unit, FC = $1,000, Units = 200
TC = ($5 × 200) + $1,000 = $2,000
High-Low Method — Step 1: Variable Cost per Unit
VC per unit = (Cost at HIGH volume − Cost at LOW volume) ÷ (HIGH units − LOW units)
📌 What it means: Find how much cost changes per unit by looking at only the busiest and slowest months.
📌 Key rule: Always pick HIGH and LOW by VOLUME (units), not by cost.
High: 7,649 units → $847 | Low: 1,149 units → $197
VC = (847 − 197) ÷ (7,649 − 1,149) = 650 ÷ 6,500 = $0.10/unit
High-Low Method — Step 2: Fixed Cost
Fixed Cost = Total Cost at any point − (VC per unit × Units at that point)
📌 What it means: Once you know the variable cost per unit, plug in either data point to back out the flat fixed amount.
📌 Tip: Use either data point — you should get the same FC either way.
FC = $847 − ($0.10 × 7,649) = $847 − $764.90 = $82.10
Contribution Margin Income Statement (5-row format)
Sales Revenue
− Variable Costs
= Contribution Margin
− Fixed Costs
= Operating Income
📌 What it means: Organizes costs by BEHAVIOR (variable vs. fixed) instead of by function (COGS vs. operating). The CM line shows how much is left to cover fixed costs after selling.
📌 Know this cold — the 5 rows are always tested.
R-Squared (R²) — Regression Goodness of Fit
R² closer to 1.0 = better fit. Want R² > 0.80 to trust the cost equation.
📌 What it means: R² tells you what % of cost changes are explained by your chosen driver. An R² of 0.90 = 90% explained, which is great. An R² of 0.50 = only 50% explained — pick a different driver.
R² = 0.93 → strong relationship, use this equation ✅
R² = 0.62 → weak relationship, find a better cost driver ❌

Ch. 3 — CVP Analysis Exam 1

Unit Contribution Margin (CM $)
CM per unit = Sales Price per unit − Variable Cost per unit
📌 What it means: How much money ONE unit "contributes" after covering its own variable costs. This goes toward paying fixed costs, then profit.
📌 Think of it as: the profit per unit BEFORE fixed costs.
eCrabb: SP = $550, VC = $300 → CM = $550 − $300 = $250
CM Ratio (CM %)
CM% = CM per unit ÷ Sales Price per unit
📌 What it means: The percentage of each dollar of revenue that goes toward covering fixed costs + profit. A 45% CM ratio means 45 cents of every $1 in sales goes to fixed costs and profit.
📌 Use this when you know sales dollars but not units.
eCrabb: CM% = $250 ÷ $550 = 45.45%
Breakeven Point in UNITS
Breakeven Units = (Fixed Costs + Desired Profit) ÷ CM per unit
📌 What it means: How many units you need to sell so revenue exactly covers all costs (profit = $0). For a target profit, just replace $0 with the target.
📌 Always round UP — you can't sell a fraction of a product.
FC = $800,000, CM = $250, Profit = $0
BE = $800,000 ÷ $250 = 3,200 units
For $50,000 profit: (800K + 50K) ÷ 250 = 3,400 units
Breakeven Point in SALES DOLLARS
Breakeven $ = (Fixed Costs + Desired Profit) ÷ CM Ratio %
📌 When to use: When the problem gives you total sales revenue instead of units, or when it's easier to work in dollars.
📌 Same logic as units version — just replace CM per unit with CM%.
FC = $800,000, CM% = 45.45%
BE$ = $800,000 ÷ 0.4545 = $1,760,000
Margin of Safety (Units & %)
MoS Units = Expected Units − Breakeven Units
MoS % = MoS Units ÷ Expected Units
📌 What it means: How far sales can DROP before you start losing money. A bigger MoS = less risky. A 30% MoS means sales could fall 30% before a loss occurs.
Expected = 4,500 units, BE = 3,200 units
MoS = 1,300 units → MoS% = 1,300 ÷ 4,500 = 28.9%
Multi-Product Breakeven (WACM)
Step 1: Sales Mix % = Units of Product ÷ Total Units of All Products
Step 2: WACM = Σ (CM per unit × Sales Mix %)
Step 3: Total BE = Fixed Costs ÷ WACM
Step 4: Each product's BE = Total BE × its Sales Mix %
📌 What it means: When selling multiple products, you need a blended "average" CM. WACM weights each product's CM by how much of total sales it represents.
📌 Round total BE up, then round each product normally.
Product A: CM=$250, mix=70% → contributes $175 to WACM
Product B: CM=$550, mix=30% → contributes $165 to WACM
WACM = $175 + $165 = $340

Ch. 4A — Product Costs & Flow of Costs Exam 2

The Three Product Cost Components
Product Cost = Direct Materials (DM) + Direct Labor (DL) + Applied MOH
📌 DM = Raw materials that physically become part of the product (e.g., lemon syrup in soda, wood in a chair).
📌 DL = Wages of workers who directly make the product (e.g., the person mixing the soda).
📌 MOH = Everything else in the factory that can't be directly traced — janitors, supervisors, factory rent, equipment depreciation.
📌 Period costs (advertising, R&D, sales commissions, CEO salary) are NEVER product costs — they go straight to the income statement.
Flow of Costs Through the Three Inventory Accounts
Raw Materials → Work In Process (WIP) → Finished Goods → Cost of Goods Sold
📌 Raw Materials: Where materials sit until you start using them.
📌 WIP: Products being made but not finished yet. DM + DL + Applied MOH are all added here.
📌 Finished Goods: Completed products waiting to be sold.
📌 COGS: Product cost moves from Finished Goods to the income statement when sold.
Total Manufacturing Costs (TMC)
DM Used + DL + Applied MOH
All product costs added to WIP this period. Use DM USED, not purchased.
Cost of Goods Manufactured (COGM)
Cost of jobs/inventory that FINISHED production this period
Transferred FROM WIP → Finished Goods. Still in WIP = not yet COGM.
Cost of Goods Available for Sale (COGAS)
Beginning Finished Goods + COGM
Total inventory that COULD be sold. Splits into: what was sold (COGS) and what wasn't (Ending FG).
Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)
Cost of inventory that was actually SOLD to customers
Moves from balance sheet (asset) → income statement (expense) when sold.

Ch. 4B — Job Order Costing Exam 2

Step 1 — Predetermined Overhead Rate (PDMOH Rate)
PDMOH Rate = Estimated (Budgeted) MOH ÷ Estimated (Budgeted) Cost Driver
📌 Calculated ONCE at the start of the year using estimates (budgeted numbers).
📌 Cost driver is usually Direct Labor Hours (DLH) or Machine Hours — it's the thing that "causes" overhead.
📌 Only include MOH costs in the numerator — NOT direct labor, NOT period costs (advertising, delivery).
Est. MOH = $26,000, Est. DLH = 8,000 hours
PDMOH Rate = $26,000 ÷ 8,000 = $3.25 per DLH
Step 2 — Apply MOH to Each Job
Applied MOH = Actual Cost Driver Used × PDMOH Rate
📌 Applied throughout the year as jobs are worked on — using the RATE calculated in Step 1 × the actual hours/machine time that specific job used.
📌 This is why it's "applied" not "actual" — we're estimating based on usage.
Job used 150 DLH × $3.25/DLH = $487.50 Applied MOH
Product Cost per Unit
Product Cost per Unit = (DM + DL + Applied MOH) ÷ Units in the Job
📌 This is the per-unit cost that goes on the balance sheet (inventory) until the item is sold, then moves to COGS.
Job: DM $8,000 + DL $4,000 + MOH $4,000 = $16,000 total
Job has 100 units → $16,000 ÷ 100 = $160/unit
Under- vs. Over-Applied MOH
Difference = Applied MOH − Actual MOH
Positive = Over-applied (jobs cost TOO MUCH)
Negative = Under-applied (jobs cost TOO LITTLE)
📌 Over-applied: We charged jobs MORE overhead than we actually spent → adjust COGS down.
📌 Under-applied: We charged jobs LESS overhead than we actually spent → adjust COGS up.
📌 In ACCT 201, always close the balance to COGS at year-end.
Applied = $30,000, Actual = $28,000
Difference = +$2,000 → Over-applied (closed to COGS as a decrease)

Ch. 7 — ABC & Alternative Costing Exam 2

Activity-Based Costing (ABC) — 5 Steps
Step 1: Identify activity pools (e.g., "Machine Setups," "Quality Inspections")
Step 2: Assign overhead costs to each pool
Step 3: Pool Rate = Pool Cost ÷ Total Cost Driver Volume
Step 4: Allocated Cost = Actual Driver Used × Pool Rate
Step 5: MOH per Unit = Total Allocated ÷ Units Produced
📌 Why it's better than one PDMOH rate: Different products use activities differently. ABC charges each product based on what it ACTUALLY uses — more accurate than spreading overhead evenly.
📌 Key difference: Traditional costing = 1 rate. ABC = multiple rates (one per activity pool).
Account Maintenance pool: $576,000 ÷ 3,000 accounts = $192/account
Basic service uses 2,000 accounts → $192 × 2,000 = $384,000 allocated
Absorption Costing
Product Cost = DM + DL + Variable MOH + FIXED MOH
Required by GAAP for external reports. Fixed MOH is IN inventory (on balance sheet) until products are sold.
Variable Costing
Product Cost = DM + DL + Variable MOH only
Fixed MOH → Period Expense (income statement)
Used internally for decisions. Fixed MOH is expensed immediately — NOT stored in inventory. NOT allowed for GAAP.
⚠️ Costing System Quick Reference: Job Order = custom/unique products (high cost per item). Process Costing = mass identical products (Coca-Cola, Legos). ABC = when products use overhead activities very differently.

Ch. 5 — Planning & Budgeting Exam 3

Standard Cost per Unit
Standard Cost = Standard Quantity (per unit) × Standard Price (per unit of input)
📌 What it means: The expected cost to make one unit under efficient conditions. Calculated separately for DM, DL, and MOH.
📌 Standard Qty = how much input ONE unit should need (including normal waste). Standard Price = expected price per unit of that input.
DM: 5 lbs per unit × $2.00/lb = $10.00 standard DM cost per unit
DL: 0.5 hrs per unit × $12.00/hr = $6.00 standard DL cost per unit
Budget Order of Preparation (Always in this order!)
1. Sales Revenue Budget → 2. Production Budget → 3. DM Purchases Budget → 4. DL Budget → 5. Cash Receipts Budget → 6. Cash Payments Budget
📌 Each budget feeds into the next. You MUST know sales before you can plan production. You MUST know production before you can plan materials purchases.
Production Budget — How many units to MAKE?
Units to Produce = Budgeted Sales Units + Desired Ending Finished Goods − Beginning Finished Goods
📌 Think of it like a T-account for Finished Goods inventory in units. You need to make enough to: (1) cover sales, (2) have a cushion at month-end, minus (3) what you already have from last month.
📌 Ending FG is usually given as a % of NEXT month's sales.
Sales = 2,000, Ending FG = 20% of Nov (3,000) = 600, Beg FG = 400
To Produce = 2,000 + 600 − 400 = 2,200 units
Direct Materials Budget — How many lbs/units of material to BUY?
DM to Purchase = (Units to Produce × DM per unit) + Desired Ending RM − Beginning RM
DM Budget ($) = DM to Purchase × Standard Price per unit of DM
📌 Same logic as Production Budget but for Raw Materials inventory.
📌 Ending RM is usually given as a % of NEXT month's production needs (not sales!).
Produce 2,200 units × 6 lbs/unit = 13,200 lbs needed
+ Ending RM 1,920 − Beg RM 1,320 = 13,800 lbs to purchase
× $0.60/lb = $8,280 DM Budget
Direct Labor Budget — How much will labor cost?
DL Budget = Units to Produce × Standard DL Hours per unit × Wage Rate per hour
2,200 units × 0.20 DLH/unit × $10/DLH = $4,400
Cash Receipts Budget — How much cash comes IN?
Cash Collected = (Prior A/R Balance) + (% of current month sales collected now) + (% of prior month sales collected now)
📌 Not all sales = cash right away. If 40% is collected in month of sale and 60% next month, you're collecting parts of TWO months' sales each month.
📌 Use the Sales Revenue Budget dollar amounts, not units.
Oct sales $24,000: collect 40% = $9,600 in Oct, $14,400 in Nov
Also collect prior A/R balance of $15,000 in Oct
Total Oct receipts = $15,000 + $9,600 = $24,600
Cash Payments Budget — How much cash goes OUT?
Cash Paid = (Prior A/P Balance) + (% of current month purchases paid now)
📌 Same idea as receipts but for purchases. If you pay 25% in month of purchase and 75% next month, you're paying parts of TWO months' purchases each month.
📌 Use DM Budget dollar amounts.
Oct purchases $8,280: pay 25% = $2,070 in Oct
Also pay prior A/P $6,000
Total Oct payments = $6,000 + $2,070 = $8,070

Ch. 6 — Variance Analysis Exam 3

The Golden Rule: Favorable vs. Unfavorable
Actual < Standard → FAVORABLE (F) — good, cost less than expected
Actual > Standard → UNFAVORABLE (U) — bad, cost more than expected
📌 Think of it from the company's perspective: paying less or using less than expected = good (Favorable). Paying more or using more than expected = bad (Unfavorable).
DM Price Variance — "Did we pay the right price for materials?"
(Actual Price − Standard Price) × Actual Quantity PURCHASED
📌 Responsibility: Purchasing department (they control prices).
📌 Key: Use Qty PURCHASED (not used) because the price is set when you buy, not when you use it.
AP = $0.55, SP = $0.50, Qty purchased = 10,000 lbs
= ($0.55 − $0.50) × 10,000 = $500 Unfavorable (paid more than expected)
⚠️ If AP > SP → Unfavorable. If AP < SP → Favorable.
DM Quantity Variance — "Did we use the right AMOUNT of materials?"
(Actual Qty USED − Standard Qty ALLOWED) × Standard Price

Standard Qty Allowed = Standard Qty per unit × Actual units produced
📌 Responsibility: Production department (they control usage/waste).
📌 Standard Qty Allowed = how much you SHOULD have used based on actual output. If you made 1,000 units and standard is 5 lbs each, you should have used 5,000 lbs.
AQ used = 4,800, SQ allowed = 5 × 1,000 = 5,000, SP = $2/lb
= (4,800 − 5,000) × $2 = −$400 → $400 Favorable (used less than expected!)
DL Rate Variance — "Did we pay the right WAGE RATE?"
(Actual Rate − Standard Rate) × Actual Hours WORKED
📌 Responsibility: HR / Management (they set wages).
📌 This is the labor version of DM Price Variance — comparing actual hourly pay to what was budgeted.
AR = $13/hr, SR = $12/hr, Actual hrs = 500
= ($13 − $12) × 500 = $500 Unfavorable (paid more per hour than planned)
DL Efficiency Variance — "Did workers take the right amount of TIME?"
(Actual Hours − Standard Hours ALLOWED) × Standard Rate

Standard Hours Allowed = Standard Hours per unit × Actual units produced
📌 Responsibility: Production supervisor (they manage worker efficiency).
📌 This is the labor version of DM Quantity Variance — comparing actual time worked to expected time.
AH = 520, SH allowed = 0.5 × 1,000 = 500, SR = $12/hr
= (520 − 500) × $12 = $240 Unfavorable (took longer than expected)
📌 The "Pitchfork" = split any variance into PRICE and QUANTITY forks. DM has 2 variances (Price + Quantity). DL has 2 variances (Rate + Efficiency). They are always tested as a pair.

Ch. 8 — Managerial Decisions & Relevant Costs Exam 4

What Makes a Cost "Relevant"?
A cost is RELEVANT if it is: (1) a FUTURE cost AND (2) DIFFERENT between alternatives
📌 Sunk costs = NEVER relevant. Money already spent cannot be changed by any decision you make today.
📌 Common/unavoidable costs = NEVER relevant. If a cost is the same no matter which option you pick, it doesn't affect the decision.
Already spent $50K on equipment → sunk cost, ignore it ❌
Extra $10K to repair that equipment → future & different → relevant ✅
Special Order Decision — Should we accept a below-normal-price order?
Accept if: Special Order Revenue > Incremental (Variable) Costs
= if Incremental CM > $0
📌 Two requirements: (1) Must have excess (unused) capacity. (2) The price must cover variable costs (positive CM).
📌 Fixed costs are usually irrelevant — they're already being paid regardless.
📌 Watch for extra costs: special packaging, shipping, or tooling = relevant and must be subtracted from CM.
Special price $80, VC $60/unit, 500 units ordered
Incremental CM = ($80 − $60) × 500 = $10,000 → ACCEPT ✅
Make vs. Buy — Should we make it ourselves or outsource?
Make cost = Avoidable costs (variable costs + any fixed costs that disappear if you stop making it)
Buy cost = Purchase price × units
Choose whichever is LOWER. Add opportunity costs if capacity can be reused.
📌 Key word: AVOIDABLE. If a fixed cost stays even when you outsource (like building rent), it's irrelevant — don't count it on the "make" side.
📌 If you could use freed-up capacity to earn profit elsewhere, that opportunity cost goes on the "make" side too.
Make: VC $8 + avoidable FC $2 = $10/unit
Buy: $9/unit → BUY (saves $1/unit) ✅
Constrained Resources — What to produce when capacity is limited?
Rank products by: CM per unit of the CONSTRAINT (not CM per unit alone)
CM per constrained unit = CM $ ÷ Units of constraint the product uses
📌 The constraint is whatever you're running out of — machine hours, labor hours, shelf space, etc.
📌 Produce as much of the highest-ranked product as possible, then move to the next.
📌 DO NOT rank by CM per unit or by profit margin % — those ignore how much of the scarce resource each product consumes.
Product X: CM $100, uses 5 machine hrs → $20 CM/hr
Product Y: CM $150, uses 10 machine hrs → $15 CM/hr
Rank: X first ($20 > $15) even though Y has higher CM per unit
Keep or Eliminate a Segment — Should we shut down a product/store/division?
KEEP if: Segment CM > Avoidable Fixed Costs of that segment
= Segment Margin is positive
ELIMINATE if: CM < Avoidable Fixed Costs (losing money that can be stopped)
📌 A segment can show a NET LOSS on the income statement but still be worth keeping if its CM covers its AVOIDABLE fixed costs AND contributes to common fixed costs.
📌 Unavoidable (common) fixed costs stay no matter what — they're irrelevant to the decision.
CM = $80,000, Avoidable FC = $60,000, Common FC = $40,000
Segment Margin = $80K − $60K = +$20,000 → KEEP ✅
(Even though net income = $80K − $100K = −$20K loss when all FC allocated)

Ch. 10 / 12 — Performance Evaluation Exam 4

Return on Investment (ROI)
ROI = Net Operating Income (NOI) ÷ Average Operating Assets

Or split into two parts:
ROI = Profit Margin % × Asset Turnover
where: Profit Margin = NOI ÷ Sales, Asset Turnover = Sales ÷ Avg Assets
📌 What it means: How many dollars of profit the division earns for every dollar of assets it uses. Higher ROI = more efficient.
📌 Why split it? Separates two problems: (1) Are we earning enough profit per sale? (2) Are we generating enough sales from our assets?
📌 Managers can improve ROI by: increasing sales, cutting costs, or reducing assets (use less capital).
NOI = $120,000, Avg Assets = $800,000
ROI = $120K ÷ $800K = 15%

Or: PM = $120K ÷ $960K sales = 12.5%, AT = $960K ÷ $800K = 1.2
ROI = 12.5% × 1.2 = 15% ✓
Residual Income (RI)
RI = NOI − (Minimum Required Rate of Return × Average Operating Assets)
📌 What it means: The dollar amount of profit ABOVE the minimum return the company requires. Positive RI = created value. Negative RI = destroyed value.
📌 Why use RI instead of ROI? ROI can discourage managers from taking good projects that would lower their division's ROI%. RI eliminates that bias — any project earning above the minimum rate increases RI.
NOI = $60,000, Min Rate = 10%, Avg Assets = $500,000
RI = $60,000 − (10% × $500,000) = $60,000 − $50,000 = $10,000 ✅
Segment Margin
Segment Margin = Contribution Margin − Traceable Fixed Costs
(Traceable = costs that ONLY exist because of this segment; would disappear if segment is eliminated)
📌 Common fixed costs (like the CEO's salary, shared building) are NOT subtracted from segment margin — they can't be avoided by closing the segment.
📌 Segment Margin is the best measure of a segment's true profitability.
The Balanced Scorecard (BSC) — 4 Perspectives
1. Financial → "Are we making money?" (ROI, RI, Revenue growth)
2. Customer → "Do customers love us?" (Satisfaction scores, retention, market share)
3. Internal Business Processes → "Are we operating efficiently?" (Cycle time, defect rates)
4. Learning & Growth → "Are we improving?" (Employee training, innovation)
📌 The BSC says financial metrics alone aren't enough — you need all 4 perspectives to get a complete picture of performance.
📌 It links strategy to daily operations.
Key Financial Ratios (Ch. 12)
Current Ratio = Current Assets ÷ Current Liabilities (liquidity — can we pay short-term bills?)
Debt-to-Equity = Total Debt ÷ Total Equity (leverage — how much debt vs. owner money?)
Gross Margin % = Gross Profit ÷ Net Sales (profitability of core product sales)
📌 Current Ratio > 1 = more current assets than current liabilities = can pay short-term debts.
📌 Higher Gross Margin % = more profitable after direct production costs — better pricing power or lower COGS.
Current Assets = $500K, Current Liabilities = $250K
Current Ratio = $500K ÷ $250K = 2.0 (healthy — $2 of current assets per $1 owed)
